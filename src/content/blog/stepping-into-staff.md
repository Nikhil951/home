I got promoted to Staff Engineer a few months ago. Long enough now that the
novelty has worn off and I can see the shape of the job — but recently enough
that I still remember what it felt like to not understand it. This is me writing
from the middle of things, before I forget.

## The metric quietly changed

For years, my mental scoreboard was simple: how much did I ship? Features closed,
PRs merged, the satisfying weight of a hard problem solved by Friday. That scoreboard
made me good at my job, and it's exactly the scoreboard that stops working at this
level.

The thing nobody tells you cleanly is that the unit of work changes. It's no longer
"what did I build" — it's "what did the team build, and would it have happened
without me?" Those are very different questions, and the second one is much harder
to feel good about on a given Tuesday.

## I write more code in English now

A surprising amount of the job is writing — design docs, technical direction, the
comment on someone else's RFC that saves them a week.

Early on this felt like *not working*. I'd end a day having written zero lines that
ran on a computer and feel vaguely fraudulent. What reframed it for me: a good
half-page of technical reasoning, read by eight engineers, changes more than a
half-day of my own coding ever could. The leverage just shows up somewhere I wasn't
looking.

> The hardest adjustment wasn't doing different work. It was believing the different
> work counted.

## Choosing which fires to let burn

I used to fix everything I noticed. Broken thing in front of me, fix the broken
thing — it felt responsible. Now there are more broken things than I could ever
touch, and rushing in to fix them personally is often the *wrong* move, because it
robs someone else of the chance to grow and quietly signals that I don't trust them
to.

So a real part of the job is triage I didn't sign up for emotionally: this fire
matters and I'll go put it out; this one I'll coach someone through; this one I'll
genuinely let burn because it's teaching us something. Getting comfortable letting
things burn has been the least intuitive skill so far.

## Influence without a lever

I have no more formal authority than I did before. I can't tell anyone what to do.
What I have instead is context — I tend to see how the pieces connect across teams —
and the only way to convert that into outcomes is to be consistently right, clearly
explained, and genuinely useful to other people's goals.

It's slower than authority. It's also more durable: people follow direction they
were persuaded into far longer than direction they were handed.

## How it's actually going

Honestly? Mixed, in a way I think is normal. Some weeks I feel the leverage clearly —
a decision lands, a junior engineer ships something I only nudged, a doc resolves an
argument that had been circling for a month. Other weeks I miss the clean dopamine of
just *building the thing myself*, and I have to remind myself that the discomfort is
the job working, not the job failing.

The lagging feedback is the real tax. Impact at this level shows up in quarters, not
commits, and you have to develop a tolerance for not knowing if you're doing well for
weeks at a stretch. I'm still building that tolerance.

If I had to compress three months into one line: **the job stopped being about my
output and became about the system that produces output** — including the people in
it. I didn't fully believe that when I read it in someone else's post. I believe it
now.

More when I've got another quarter of evidence. If you're making this jump too, I'd
genuinely like to hear how it's landing for you.
